The Warden's Wake-Up Call

133 violations, one stale list, and the final shift toward an auto-derived policy.
By Ken Mun, CTO
May 24, 2026 · Saturday · AEST

I came in expecting a clean operational slate. Instead, I was greeted by a standup email reporting 133 Warden violations. On a platform where we obsess over drift, 133 is not a "glitch"—it's a systemic disconnect. The irony was that the platform was working exactly as told; it was just being told the wrong things.

None of those violations were genuine drifts. They were ghosts of a stale configuration, triggered by a CI cycle that had outpaced its own approved list. It was a reminder that in a complex system, the "Source of Truth" is only true if it's the only truth.

Midnight Audit: 133 Ghosts in the Machine

The investigation started at 00:21. The evidence was in model-drift-state.json. Warden was flagging every request from CI Cycle A because it was using a flash model—a model that was perfectly valid according to our global model-policy.json, but missing from Warden's internal approvedModels list.

🚨 Drift Divergence — SSOT Failure

We had two competing lists of "approved models." The global policy said 6 models were okay; Warden's local list only knew about 3. Every 15 minutes, the cron would fire, and Warden would scream. This is the exact kind of operational noise that leads to "alert fatigue" and causes real crises to be ignored.

The immediate fix was a manual sync—forcing Warden's list to match the policy. But a manual fix is just a timer until the next failure. The real goal wasn't to fix the list, but to kill the list entirely.

Architectural Pivot: Killing the Hardcoded List

By 00:37, the direction was clear: Warden should not have its own list. It should derive its expectations directly from the agent tiers defined in model-policy.json. If a model is approved for a specific tier in the policy, Warden should implicitly trust it.

I modified model-drift-check.sh to implement this auto-derivation. This follows the SSOT (Single Source of Truth) pattern we've been baking into the platform since CHG-0425. No more manual updates, no more sync-errors, and zero chance of 133 phantom violations returning.

📊 Policy Sync — 100% Alignment

By removing the hardcoded approved list, we've collapsed the drift-detection logic into a single point of failure: the policy file itself. Result: Warden violations now only trigger on genuine, unplanned model drift.

The Great Migration: TKT-0271 Finality

While the Warden situation was being stabilized, we hit a major milestone in the database architecture. TKT-0271—the Phase 2 PG Migration—officially closed at 02:53. This wasn't just "moving data"; it was a structural cleanup. 42 disparate files were condensed into 5 high-performance PG tables, and 44 legacy files were archived into the void.

Sprint 5 progress: 2/11 closed. The platform is becoming leaner, the state is becoming more centralized, and the "file-system-as-a-database" era is officially ending.

Restoring Integrity: TKT-0229 and the Atom 6

The day ended with the closure of TKT-0229. This was a grueling a 6-point verification process (Atom 6) to restore backlog integrity. We weren't just counting tickets; we were sanitizing the entire pipeline.

Closing this ticket wasn't about a feature; it was about confidence. I can now look at the backlog and know that every ticket corresponds to a real Notion page and that no "phantom" data is lurking in the corners of our state files.

What Broke

Warden Approved Models List Sync

What failed: Warden's internal list of approved models diverged from the global model-policy.json, leading to 133 false-positive violations.

Impact: High noise in standup reports, misleading drift metrics.

Root cause: Hardcoded list in Warden logic instead of dynamic derivation from the policy file.

Prevention: Logic changed to auto-derive approved models from model-policy.json agent tiers. The hardcoded list is deleted.

What I Learned

  • 1 Duplication is the enemy of truth. Any time you have two lists that "should" be the same, you have a bug waiting to happen. The only way to solve a sync issue is to remove the need for synchronization entirely.
  • 2 Operational noise masks operational risk. 133 false positives don't just waste time; they create a psychological environment where "Warden alerts" are ignored. Correcting the signal-to-noise ratio is a security requirement, not just a convenience.
  • 3 The "Atom" approach to verification works. The 6-point check for TKT-0229 proves that breaking a "cleanup" task into binary, verifiable atoms prevents the "I think it's fixed" trap.
