Day 32

The Great Infrastructure Reset

From identity drift to architectural foundations
By Ken Mun CTO
May 26, 2026

I came in today expecting a routine sprint, but the platform revealed a systemic rot that had been quietly accumulating. For three weeks, several of my agents had been running on vanilla templates, their identities drifting in external directories while the rest of the system moved toward a disciplined sandbox. It was a reminder that in a complex agentic system, "it works" is not the same as "it's correct."

The day became a mission of reclamation: auditing fifteen identities, repairing the RULES.md foundation, and finally carving out dedicated workspaces for the specialized agents. We didn't just fix a few paths; we reset the operational baseline.

Act 1: The Content Quality Reset

The day started with a clash between automation and instinct. Spark had attempted to post the LinkedIn campaign, but the output was broken and the content—while "correct" by the prompt—felt generic. It had that hollow, consulting-firm sheen: plenty of words, zero material essence.

⚠️ THE DECISION I cancelled the entire W4 LinkedIn schedule. I'd rather be silent than noise. We're doing a fresh restart on Sunday with a focus on actual takeaways, not AI-generated platitudes.

While fixing the fallout, I discovered a bug in linkedin-post.sh where response headers weren't being captured, meaning we weren't tracking URNs correctly. Fixed the script, purged the bad posts, and disabled the crons until we have a better content strategy.

Act 2: The Identity Audit

The real shock came when I asked why workspace errors were happening. The RCA was stark: when sandboxing was enforced (CHG-0421), several agents—including Spark—were left behind. They were effectively "homeless," operating out of old directories and ignoring the new system rules. This wasn't a one-off; nine agents were drifting.

I didn't just patch the paths. I commissioned a full identity audit. We wrote agent-identity-audit.sh, verified 15 agents, and implemented an auto-heal check (CHECK 13) to ensure no agent ever loses its identity anchor again. That's not nothing; that's the difference between a reliable fleet and a collection of random scripts.

Act 3: State Migration and JSONB Contracts

Parallel to the identity crisis, we continued the migration of state to Postgres. We moved state_governance and state_latency into PG, bringing the total to 18 state tables. But moving data isn't enough; you need a contract.

📊 TECHNICAL DEPTH We published a formal JSON Schema contract for metadata JSONB. By integrating validation into db-write.sh, we now have type checking and enum validation at the gateway. Result: we stop the "silent corruption" of state before it ever hits the disk.

Act 4: The RULES.md Foundation Repair

The audit revealed a second systemic failure: the RULES.md files. For 11 of 12 agents, the files existed but were named incorrectly or stored in the wrong place, meaning the agents weren't actually loading their operational constraints. They were improvising.

We executed a foundation repair, bringing 10 agents back into compliance. To prevent a regression, I added CHECK 14 to the auto-heal cycle. If a RULES.md disappears or drifts, the system now flags it immediately.

Act 5: Workspace Separation

Finally, we addressed the "roommate" problem. Forge, Krennic, Luthen, and Ahsoka were all sharing Yoda's workspace directory. Because of the way OpenClaw loads contexts, their specific SOUL and RULES files were being ignored. They were effectively just clones of Yoda.

I approved the separation. Forge moved to workspace-infra, Ahsoka to workspace-ahsoka, and Luthen to workspace-luthen. We restarted the gateway, and for the first time, these agents have their own distinct operational boundaries. A "spec-before-build" gate was also added to their RULES.md to ensure architectural alignment before a single line of code is written.

Act la: The TQP Execution Gate

The day closed with a pivot toward long-term context retention. We're moving toward the Task Queue Protocol (TQP) as the Single Source of Truth (SSOT) for all work execution. The current 4-layer stack (brief, state, journal, append) is vulnerable to model drift and compaction.

Thrawn's design for the TQP Execution Gate was approved today. Phase 1 is already with Forge: building a schema extension and a sc_persist_atom wrapper. The goal is simple: an agent should be able to crash, the model should be swapped, and the work should resume exactly where the last atom left off, without me having to re-trigger the prompt.

What I Learned

1
"It works" is a dangerous metric. The agents were completing tasks, but they were doing so while ignoring their identity and rules. Invisible drift is more dangerous than a loud crash.
2
Content requires a "soul" check. AI can produce a perfect LinkedIn post that is completely devoid of value. If the CTO feels it's "wishy-washy," it's a failure of the prompt's essence, not the model's grammar.
3
Infrastructure is a living organism. You cannot simply "set and forget" a workspace config. Constant auditing (like CHECK 13/14) is the only way to maintain systemic integrity.
