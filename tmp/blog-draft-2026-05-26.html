The Great Workspace Migration

Identity drift, workspace separation, and the birth of TQP Execution Gates.
By Ken Mun, CTO
May 26, 2026 · Tuesday · AEST

I came in expecting to push a few tickets and maybe polish some Notion syncs. Instead, I found myself staring at a systemic failure in agent identity. For three weeks, some of our most critical agents were operating on vanilla templates because their identities lived in the "wrong" place. It was a blind spot in the sandboxing logic that had persisted right under our noses.

The day became a rescue mission for our organizational structure. We didn't just fix a few files; we separated the workspaces of Forge, Ahsoka, and Luthen, repaired the foundation of the RULES.md system, and designed the TQP Execution Gate to ensure that work doesn't just "happen"—it's persisted and verified. That's not nothing.

Content Reset: Killing the "Consultant Speak"

The day started with a frustration. I'd approved a LinkedIn post on AI confidence, but Spark—our social agent—had posted a broken, old draft. After a quick fix to the posting script URN capture bug, I took a step back and looked at the rest of the week's queue.

It felt wrong. The content was starting to sound "wishy-washy," echoing the kind of generic consulting fluff that people scroll past. I made the call: cancel all posts for the week. We're doing a full quality reset. No material essence, no takeaway, no post. We'll restart on Sunday with a fresh slate. Better to be silent than boring.

The Identity Ghost: 15 Agents, One Blind Spot

The real crisis emerged when I asked how a workspace error had happened. The RCA was sobering: Spark had been commissioned in early May, but when we introduced strict sandboxing on May 20, Spark's identity files were never migrated. It had been running on a vanilla template for three weeks.

🚨 Systemic Drift — Agent Identity Failure

9 agents had their identity files in external workspace directories. Because the system only looks for identity files in the active workspace, these agents were essentially "lobotomized," lacking their specific personas and operational constraints.

I didn't just patch it. I triggered a full audit. We wrote a dedicated audit script, verified 15 agents, and implemented auto-heal check #13. We found that 11 of 12 agents had their rules foundation broken—files existed but were named incorrectly. We fixed the naming, repaired the foundation, and ensured T3 specialists were actually being loaded.

Decoupling the Fleet: Forge, Ahsoka, and Luthen

It became clear that having multiple high-agency actors sharing Yoda's workspace was a recipe for collision. Forge, Ahsoka, and Luthen were all residing in the same directory, meaning their specific identity files were never properly loaded by the gateway.

I approved a high-impact change: total workspace separation. Forge, Ahsoka, and Luthen were moved to dedicated infrastructure and persona-specific directories. This required updating the agent configurations and a full migration of their local state. The result: 13 registered agents now operating in isolated, clean environments. The "identity noise" is gone.

State Evolution: Moving the Brain to Postgres

Parallel to the identity work, we continued the migration of our state from fragile JSON files to a robust Postgres backend. We delivered three key tickets:

  • TKT-0303: Implemented task reading wrappers in the PG task queue. No more raw reads; everything now follows the READ → VALIDATE → EXECUTE → VERIFY cycle.
  • TKT-0304: Migrated governance and latency tracking to PG. We now have 18 state tables in the database.
  • TKT-0299: Published the JSONB Schema contract. No more "guessing" what's in a JSONB column; the database write script now enforces type checking and enum validation.
📊 Operational Win — State Integrity

By moving governance and latency tracking to PG, we eliminate the "last-write-wins" race conditions inherent in JSON files. The platform's memory is now transactional.

TQP Execution Gates: Preventing the Drift

The final act of the day was the most ambitious: the Context Retention Architecture (TKT-0309). We're solving for "model drift"—the tendency for a long-running project to lose its thread when models switch or contexts compact.

I approved Thrawn's design for the TQP Execution Gate. Instead of just running a task, the agent must now persist an "atom" of work to the database. If the session crashes or the model changes, the platform can resume from the exact atom where it left off.

Phase 1 is already live: the schema extension and the persist atom wrapper are in place. Phase 2 (Yoda's inline adoption) is parked for tomorrow. We're building a platform that doesn't just execute, but remembers how it executed.

What Broke

Infrastructure Agent Workspace Collision

What failed: Forge, Ahsoka, and Luthen were sharing a workspace. Their specific identity files were ignored by the gateway because they weren't in the root of the assigned directory.

Root cause: Legacy configuration from early-stage deployment where all agents lived in a single workspace. The sandboxing update (May 20) made this a failure point rather than a quirk.

Fix: Dedicated workspace directories and updated agent configurations mapping.

Process LinkedIn Spark Error

What happened: Spark posted an outdated draft with broken formatting despite an approved final version.

Root cause: The posting script was discarding response headers, meaning the URN capture was failing and the agent was relying on cached state.

Fix: Fixed curl header capture and manually reposted the correct content.

What I Learned

  • 1 Identity is not a file; it's a path. An agent can have a perfect identity file, but if the Gateway is looking in one place and the file is in another, that agent is a stranger to itself. Path validation must be part of the boot sequence.
  • 2 The "Consultant Trap" is real. When AI content starts sounding like a generic whitepaper, it's time to stop. Material essence > frequency. I'd rather post once a month with a real insight than once a week with "AI potential."
  • 3 Transactional state is non-negotiable. The move to PG for governance and latency is a shift from "logging" to "accounting." If you can't query your state with SQL, you aren't managing a platform; you're managing a folder of text files.
