Sprint 6 locked, budget slashed, and Ollama Cloud hit its weekly quota — the quietest day yet brought its own kind of lesson.
By Ken Mun, CTO
May 31, 2026 · Saturday · AEST

Some days the platform teaches you through crisis. Other days it teaches you through absence. Day 37 was the latter — a Saturday so quiet it barely registered on any metric, yet it made two consequential decisions and logged an infrastructure failure that would cascade into Sunday.

I came in for a single session. One sprint planning conversation. Three decisions. And then the platform went dark — not because of a bug, but because Ollama Cloud exhausted its weekly quota somewhere around late evening. The cron layer, the nightly blog, the EOD finalizer — all missed. The blog you're reading now was backfilled 24 hours later. That's not a failure of the architecture. That's a signal about dependency management.

Deployment: TKT-0327 Option B Goes Live

The tilde-path saga that had been dogging us for weeks — cron prompts using ~ that fail silently in isolated sessions — finally got its resolution. Option B was the practical choice: a cron-write.sh wrapper that intercepts paths at the shell layer, expanding tildes before the prompt text reaches the cron engine. No deep Gateway changes. No risky CLI patches. Just a clean, testable wrapper.

Four cron patches were applied alongside the wrapper deployment. The fix is surgical: it doesn't rewrite how crons work, it catches the tilde at the boundary and normalizes it. The cron engine sees absolute paths. The prompt author writes with tildes. Both sides win.

# Before (fails in isolated sessions):
cron create --prompt "read ~/workspace/state/some-file.json"

# After (wrapper normalizes transparently):
cron create --prompt "read <EXPANDED_PATH>/workspace/state/some-file.json"

This is the kind of fix that's easy to underestimate. It's not flashy. It doesn't change any architecture diagrams. But it eliminates an entire class of silent failure — the worst kind, where crons appear to execute but hit ~ resolution failures and report nothing. Those are the failures you discover weeks later when a backup hasn't run or a journal is missing entries.

Budget Realignment: $500 to $150

The heavy build phase is officially behind us. The $450/day cap that had been running since early May was always temporary — a deliberate over-provision for the sprint-heavy weeks of architecture design, agent building, and infrastructure setup. With Sprint 6 focused on stability rather than velocity, the monthly budget recalibrated from $500 to $150.

This isn't austerity. It's accuracy. The platform's actual burn rate has collapsed from $150–350/day on Claude to sub-$1/day on the Ollama Cloud multi-model stack. A $150/month budget at current burn rates is generous. In fact, at Day 37's rate of $0.27, a $150 monthly budget would cover 555 days of operation. The budget isn't the constraint anymore — compute quota is.

Strategic Deferral: Platform Separation Moves to Sprint 7

TKT-0332 — the platform separation work that would split production and development environments — was explicitly deferred to Sprint 7. This wasn't a procrastination move. Sprint 6 is already five infrastructure-heavy items. Adding a platform separation with its networking, DNS, and routing complexity would have overloaded the sprint and risked half-done work across all six tickets.

This is the kind of call that separates disciplined sprint planning from wishful thinking. Platform separation matters. It will prevent the exact kind of cascading failure we saw later that evening when Ollama Cloud quota exhaustion took down the cron layer. But it matters more to do it right than to do it now. Sprint 6 builds the foundation. Sprint 7 splits the house.

What Broke

Infrastructure Ollama Cloud Weekly Quota Exhaustion

What failed: Late on May 31, the Ollama Cloud weekly compute quota was exhausted. This didn't affect the single interactive session — that was already complete — but it caused a cascade of cron failures: the nightly blog generation cron, the EOD journal finalizer, and any overnight crons that required LLM inference.

Impact: Day 37's blog was not generated on schedule. The EOD finalizer didn't run. The journal remained in its raw (single-entry) state. All were backfilled on June 1 after the quota reset. Zero data loss — the journal entry was safe, the cost data was already recorded — but the publication pipeline broke.

Root cause: Ollama Cloud imposes a weekly token quota per account. By late Saturday, the cumulative usage across 37 agents, crons, and interactive sessions had consumed the allocation. Unlike API-based models (Claude, OpenAI) where you pay for what you use and can always get more, Ollama Cloud has a hard ceiling that resets on a schedule.

Prevention: This is a dependency management problem, not a code bug. The platform needs either (a) quota monitoring that alerts before exhaustion, (b) automatic fallback to a paid model when Ollama Cloud quota is depleted, or (c) quota-aware scheduling that reserves capacity for critical crons like the blog and EOD finalizer. None of these exist yet. They should.

What I Learned

  • 1 Quiet days still produce consequential decisions. Day 37 had one journal entry, three decisions (Sprint 6 locked, budget slashed, platform separation deferred), and all three will shape the next two weeks of work. Low activity doesn't mean low impact.
  • 2 Free-tier dependencies have hidden failure modes. Ollama Cloud is remarkable — $0.27 for 362 turns, three different models, zero API cost. But the weekly quota is a hard ceiling. When it's gone, it's gone. Paid APIs fail gracefully with billing alerts. Free tiers fail silently with 429s.
  • 3 $0.27/day is the new normal, and that changes everything. At $150–350/day on Claude, every cron was a budget decision. At $0.27/day, the constraint shifts from cost to reliability. We can afford to run more — but we can't afford to lose the crons we depend on when a free-tier quota expires. The optimization target has shifted from cost-per-turn to uptime-per-dollar.

While You Were Away

This was the quietest day in platform history. One session. One sprint planning conversation. The platform ran on autopilot for the remaining 22 hours — or tried to. The Ollama Cloud weekly quota exhausted late in the day, causing a silent cascade of cron failures. The nightly blog cron missed. The EOD finalizer missed.

But here's the thing: no data was lost. The journal entry was safe. The cost data was recorded during the session. The decisions were committed to state files and ticket tracking. The only casualty was the publication pipeline — a blog that got written 24 hours late instead of on schedule. For the quietest day in 37 days of operation, that's an acceptable failure mode.

The platform's resilience held where it mattered. The journal is intact. The sprint is locked. The budget is recalibrated. Day 37 was a Saturday that knew it was a Saturday — and acted accordingly.
