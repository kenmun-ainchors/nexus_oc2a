Day 25

The Silent Tuesday

Autopilot mode, rate-limit stalls, and the persistence of version drift.
Ken Mun (CTO)

I came in expecting a day of steady progress, but Tuesday decided to be a ghost town. There was no activity from Angie—no emails, no messages, no updates on the onboarding pipeline. It was one of those days where the system's autonomous rhythms became the primary narrative. When the platform is quiet, you notice the friction in the machine.

The shape of the day was a long stretch of silence punctuated by a single, sharp intervention. It wasn't a crisis, but it was a reminder that even the "cloud" has ceilings. We hit a rate limit, stalled a critical cycle, and had to pivot models on the fly to get back to green.

Act 1: The Vacuum of Activity

For the better part of the day, the business stream was flat. No updates on the HRDF API keys, no debriefs from the May 15 meeting, and a continuing silence from Angie since early May. In a high-velocity build phase, this kind of silence can be deceptive—it feels like a break, but it's actually a backlog growing in the dark.

While the human side of the operation was dormant, OpenClaw was humming. Crons fired, logs were aggregated, and the platform maintained its state. It's a testament to the stability of the current runtime that I can step away for hours and find the system exactly where I left it, provided the models don't decide to stop talking.

Act 2: The Rate-Limit Wall

Mid-afternoon, the silence was broken by a failure in CI Cycle A. The system flagged that the run was stuck. The culprit? gemma4:31b-cloud had hit a rate limit on Ollama Pro. It wasn't a crash, but a stall—the kind of subtle failure that doesn't trigger a P1 alert but stops the clock on validation.

I didn't want to wait for the window to reset. I gave the order: "yes, force-clear and retry now". Yoda killed the hanging process and pivoted the run to deepseek-v4-flash:cloud.

📊 Decision: Model Pivot The move to the flash model wasn't just about speed; it was about bypassing the specific rate-limit bucket associated with the larger Gemma model. Result: CI Cycle A completed successfully within minutes.

Act 3: The Version Drift Ghost

The successful run of Cycle A brought a familiar warning back to the surface: Version Drift. The system is running v2026.5.12, but v2026.5.18 is available. It's a persistent shadow in the logs—a reminder that the OC1 upgrade (TRIGGER-04) is still sitting on the backlog.

It didn't stop the work today, but it's a technical debt that needs to be cleared. We're operating on a slightly outdated core while the surrounding tools continue to evolve. That's not nothing. It's a risk factor that will eventually demand a dedicated window of downtime to resolve.

What I Learned

The Danger of the "Quiet Day": Silence from stakeholders isn't a lack of work; it's an accumulation of dependency. The longer the gap in communication, the harder the restart will be once the floodgates open.

Model Redundancy is Operational Insurance: Being able to pivot from a rate-limited 31B model to a Flash model in seconds is the difference between a stalled pipeline and a completed cycle. The multi-model strategy isn't just for "quality"—it's for availability.
