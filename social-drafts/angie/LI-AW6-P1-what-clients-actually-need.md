## Draft

---

I used to think clients came to me for AI knowledge. They do not. They come because they are drowning in tools and need someone to make the water shallow enough to stand in.

The first time a client told me they had tried five different AI tools and none of them worked, I assumed the tools were the problem. They were not. The client had never been taught how to match a tool to a job. They had been sold features, not outcomes. And when the feature did not magically produce the outcome, they moved to the next tool.

This is the real gap in AI training right now. Not a shortage of tools. A shortage of judgment about which tool fits which task, and how to tell the difference.

Here is what I changed in my sessions.

I stopped teaching prompts and started teaching workflow design. The prompt is the last step, not the first. Before you ask anything of an AI, you need to know what the work actually is, where the judgment lives, and what the human still owns.

I started every session with one question: what is the decision you are avoiding by bringing in an AI? The answer tells me everything. If they are avoiding a judgment call, the AI cannot help. If they are avoiding repetitive execution, the AI can. Most people cannot tell the difference.

I added a module I never thought I would need: how to read AI output like a reviewer, not a consumer. Most users treat generated text as received wisdom. I teach them to treat it as a first draft that needs a human editor with domain knowledge. The editor is them. The AI does not have it.

The shift from tool training to judgment training is the shift from making people capable of using AI to making them capable of working with it. Those are different things. The first produces users. The second produces operators.

My clients do not need more tools. They need clearer thinking about the ones they already have.
