I have been running training sessions on AI for a while now. Through that work I have learned that the things I stopped doing matter as much as the things I started.

Here is what I stopped.

Trusting the tool to be careful. I used to assume the AI would handle nuance. It does not. Now I design every session so the AI cannot mislead. The safety is in the structure, not the tool.

Patching training materials at the edges. I used to update a slide here, a prompt there. Now I rewrite the module when it is wrong. Small edits compound confusion. Rewrites fix the root.

Assuming the client understood because they nodded. I used to think comprehension meant agreement. Now I check. Looking engaged is not the same as being ready.

Optimising prompts for the session. I used to spend time refining what I asked the AI in front of the room. Now I optimise the material that feeds the session. The prompt is downstream of the prep.

Pushing through when the material was broken. I used to keep going because the room was waiting. Now I stop, fix, then resume. A broken session delivered faster is still broken.

Here is what I started.

Treating training materials like production code. Versioned, tested, auditable. The material is the system.

Measuring comprehension per module. Every session has a check. Every learner has a ceiling I respect. I know what goes in.

Running regular audits of my own content. Small ones, after every delivery. Not annual reviews. Immediate checks.

Building governance into the session design. Not in a handbook. Handbooks sit on shelves. Design actually stops things from going wrong.

Asking what the learning outcome is before I ask what the prompt is. This sounds small. It changed everything.

The shift from prompt chasing to outcome design is the shift from treating AI as a demo to treating it as a capability.

Demos impress. Capabilities last.
