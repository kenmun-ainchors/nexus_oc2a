## Draft

---

I used to build beautiful slide decks. Every module had a visual identity, consistent fonts, a colour palette. They looked professional. Clients commented on them.

The decks were also the thing holding the training back.

Here is why. A slide deck is a promise that the content is settled. Once it is designed and formatted, it becomes expensive to change. And in AI training, the content needs to change constantly. Models shift. Capabilities change. What was true last quarter is not true now.

I watched myself spend more time updating slides than updating the thinking behind them. The deck was becoming the product, and the product was becoming stale.

So I stopped building decks. Now my materials are modular documents that change quickly. One concept per page. No design dependency. If a model changes, I rewrite one page, not thirty slides. If a client brings a new use case, I add one page, not a new section.

The result is unexpected. Clients do not miss the slides. They barely notice they are gone. What they notice is that the material matches the moment. It is current. It is specific. It does not feel like a generic session that has been delivered many times before.

The discipline is harder. Slides give you structure for free. Modular documents force you to build the structure yourself, every time. You have to know the material deeply enough to hold it without visual scaffolding.

That is the point. If I need thirty slides to teach a concept, I do not understand the concept well enough to teach it.

The best sessions I have run recently have no slides at all. Just a shared document, a clear framework, and a room full of real questions. The energy is different. The learning is sharper. Nobody leaves with a handout they will never open. They leave with a workflow they built during the session.

That is the trade-off. Beautiful decks create passive audiences. Lean materials create active participants. I chose the second.
