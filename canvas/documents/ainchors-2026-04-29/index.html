Day 5: The Day We Went External

Days 1 through 4 were entirely internal. Infrastructure, governance, resilience, agents — all pointed inward. Day 5 was when we produced the first thing meant to be seen by the outside world: a company profile PDF for AInchor Solutions Pty Ltd.

It didn't come out perfect on the first try. Page two broke, the first draft was blank, and the web-to-Telegram handoff surfaced a bug in the /resume command that I hadn't caught before. We fixed all of it — in the same session. That's the pattern I want to set: surface the problem, fix it immediately, move on.

Alongside the company profile, we cleared three critical carry-over items from Day 4, groomed the Research Framework, approved the SLA reporting approach, and finished the day by connecting Google Workspace. By 9 PM, the platform had another meaningful capability layer it didn't have in the morning.

Sprint Block 1 · 10:45–11:35 AM

I came online with a simple directive: fix Aria's fallback chain first, then clear the critical backlog. After the fallback fix landed in minutes, I gave the three-item backlog a single go-ahead: "lets do all the critical ones. go".

Three tickets raised, three sub-agents spawned simultaneously:

The sub-agent completed the PIR, wrote the lessons-learned section, and formally closed the loop. Lesson: any plugin that crash-loops every 9 seconds needs to be disabled before it takes the gateway down.

The cost-tracker.sh script's alert logic was broken — the WARNING_TIER1 check wasn't firing correctly against cost-alert-state.json. The parser was patched, alert logic verified against all three tiers ($80, $40, $15). Credit alerts now fire reliably.
Sub-Agent 3 — US23
Resilient Outage Handling
This was the big one. Built the full outage handling stack: scripts/outage-handler.sh, scripts/validate-fallback-chain.sh (cron: every hour), state/standby-mode.json structure, and the Operations/GatewayRecovery.md runbook. The platform can now auto-detect billing and auth failures, validate the fallback chain on boot and first failure, and display a user-facing standby banner if Anthropic goes down.

All three items closed in a single parallel dispatch. No manual back-and-forth required. That's the sub-agent model working as intended.

Sprint Block 2 · 2:38–5:00 PM

First External Asset: The Company Profile PDF

I asked Yoda to produce a 2-page company profile for AInchor Solutions Pty Ltd. This was the first time we'd tried to produce something meant for an external audience — not infrastructure, not a script, not a state file. A document someone else might actually read.

It didn't work first go. The PDF came back blank. I asked for a regeneration, it sent to my Gmail (kenmun@gmail.com), but when I switched to Telegram and used /resume, the handoff summary had no idea the company profile had been built. The web session context wasn't being picked up.

Two Bugs, Fixed Same Session

  • Bug 1 — Blank PDF / broken page 2: PDF generation was failing silently. Regenerated with explicit path output. Page 2 layout was then broken on the second version — fixed layout, confirmed render.
  • Bug 2 — /resume cross-channel gap: /resume was only reading the Telegram session. It had no visibility into the webchat session where the PDF sprint happened. Patched /resume to scan both agent:main:telegram and agent:main:webchat sessions and synthesise a unified handoff.

The final company profile was rewritten to be technically forward — governance layer (Warden, Shield, Lex, Sage), resilience frameworks (auto-heal, fallback chain, outage handler), and agile delivery (ITSM ticketing, US-driven backlog, sub-agent execution). That's what AInchor Solutions is. The brochure now reflects it.

Lesson learned: any new channel added to the platform (Signal, WhatsApp, etc.) needs the same treatment in /resume logic. Cross-channel context stitching is now a requirement, not an afterthought.

Sprint Block 3 · 4:58–6:09 PM

US20: Research Framework Groomed

The Research Framework has been in the backlog since Day 3. We groomed it properly today. I reviewed four options for how to formalise the research capability:

  • Option 1: Lightweight — prompt template + research checklist in AGENTS.md
  • Option 2: Full framework — dedicated research runner, structured output, Obsidian integration
  • Option 3: Sub-agent pattern — spawn isolated research agents per query
  • Option 4: Extend existing AKB — add a new research section to the knowledge base

I approved all four with Option 4 as the implementation path: we extend the existing Obsidian + Notion AKB rather than building a parallel system. Decision locked and logged. US20 is groomed and ready for the next sprint.

Sprint Block 4 · 8:24–8:41 PM

US18: Monthly SLA Report Approved and Running

US18 — Monthly SLA Report — has been in the backlog since Day 2. Today it got a brief, a decision, and a sub-agent dispatch.

Sub-agent dispatched under TKT-0024. April 2026 SLA report (partial month, Days 1–29) underway. We'll have the first formal reliability report before the month closes.

Reflection

Day 5 Takeaway

Day 5 was when the platform produced its first external-facing output. The company profile PDF is nothing groundbreaking on its own — but it represents a shift in what we're building toward. The infrastructure days are mostly done. Now we're starting to use it.

The bugs that surfaced today — blank PDF, broken page 2, cross-channel handoff gap — are the kind of bugs that only appear when you actually try to ship something. They're good bugs to find in Day 5 rather than Day 50. We found them, fixed them, and added guards to catch the equivalent failure next time.

The autonomous overnight run continues to prove its value. The platform ran four scheduled tasks while I slept. By the time I came online, the system had already done its housekeeping. That's the compounding return on the infrastructure investment from Days 1–4.

Google Workspace is connected. SLA reporting is in motion. Research Framework is groomed. We're not just building infrastructure anymore — we're building capability.
