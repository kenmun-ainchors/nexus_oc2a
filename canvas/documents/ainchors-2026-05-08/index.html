AInchors Build Log · Day 14

The Governance Day

19 changes. Platform infrastructure complete. Agent governance formalised. Architecture locked. The day we stopped building and started governing — and it made all the difference.

📅 Friday, 8 May 2026 ✍️ Ken Mun, CTO — AInchors 🔁 19 CHGs (CHG-0225 → CHG-0243) ⏱ ~20 hours

I came in expecting to close a few loose ends before the weekend. What I got instead was something I didn't plan for but absolutely needed: a day of reckoning with the governance and architecture decisions we'd been deferring since day one.

By the time I called it at 10 PM, we'd raised 19 change records, formalised a 5-tier agent governance model, locked the P1–P4 phase definitions (including a significant reframe of P3), confirmed that Anthropic's DPA blocks client data on Claude API, and formalised a Definition of Done that will govern every delivery from Sprint 2 onwards. None of those were on the plan. All of them were necessary.

That's not nothing. That's actually everything.

Act 1 — 01:19 AEST

The 1AM Wake-Up Call

I was asleep. Warden wasn't. At 1:19 AM, the model compliance cron detected that Shield, Lex, and Sage — our governance triad — had drifted from Haiku back to Sonnet. Again. Yoda remediated automatically before I was awake, logged CHG-0225, and confirmed clean.

This is exactly why we built Warden. The drift was silent, the fix was silent, and I only know about it because the escalation file exists. That's the system working. The lesson I keep learning: automation without observability is just hope. We have both now.

Act 2 — 08:00–12:31 AEST

Morning Sprint: Remote Access, Cron Fixes, and a Baseline Cleanup

The morning was infrastructure. Three things I'd been meaning to do for a week.

First: Tailscale Serve. The OpenClaw gateway was already bound to loopback (S2 compliance — never expose the port publicly). But I couldn't reach it from anywhere except OC1 itself. Tailscale Serve changed that. Now https://ainchorss-mac-mini.tail5e2567.ts.net gives me the full gateway from any device on my Tailscale mesh. S2 stays intact. Problem solved.

Second: incomplete_turns. A handful of crons were completing without a proper terminal signal — the gateway would keep the session open, burning tokens waiting for a response that wasn't coming. We applied a four-fix bundle: Haiku fallbacks on 7 Sonnet crons, explicit timeout values, clearer terminal signals for the governance triad, and a Warden schedule stagger to avoid top-of-hour collisions. Embarrassingly simple fixes. Should have done this two weeks ago.

Third: Shield/Lex/Sage back to Haiku. After the Warden incident this morning, I confirmed with the system that this is the right call for pre-OC2. Sonnet is 3.75× the cost of Haiku per million tokens. Our governance triad runs 96 times a day. The math is obvious. They move to Gemma4 local after OC2 arrives (TRIGGER-03). Until then: Haiku.

Act 3 — 13:41–14:23 AEST

Credit Policy + The Agent Governance Framework

Two separate things in the early afternoon that both come down to the same underlying discipline: knowing the rules before you need them.

On credits: I'd been operating with the wrong mental model of the auto-reload policy. I thought a reload event topped up to $500. It doesn't. It adds $450 when balance hits $50 — bringing total to $500. The difference matters for alert calibration. If you're watching for a $15 emergency that can't happen until the $50 reload fails, your Tier 3 threshold is set to the wrong thing. Fixed. The new thresholds:

📊 Credit Alert Tiers (recalibrated)
T1 ($60): Alert once. Auto-reload imminent. Continuous runway likely.
T2 ($55): Pre-reload heads-up. Verify billing card is active.
T3 ($15): Auto-reload failed. Real emergency. Pause everything.

On governance: Atlas completed TKT-0103 — an audit of the agent fleet against a governance framework that didn't formally exist yet. The finding was: we had the right instincts, but no written model. I reviewed Atlas's recommendations and approved all 7 decisions. The result is the Agent Governance Framework v1.0, a 5-tier model that covers every agent we have and every agent we'll build.

Act 4 — 15:00–15:19 AEST

P3 Was Never a Build Phase

This one changed the roadmap. We'd been treating P3 (SaaS with company/multi-agent shared context) as a separate build phase — its own sprint capacity, its own timeline, its own gate. It's not. It never was.

The TKT-0104 Data+Memory Architecture session forced the question: when exactly do you build multi-tenant? The answer is from day one in P2. You add tenant_id, you implement RLS (row-level security) at the database layer, you build the shared state architecture. You do this regardless of whether P3 is in the roadmap — because retrofitting multi-tenancy after the fact is the kind of thing that kills SaaS products mid-growth.

P3 as a commercial tier is just a feature unlock. When enough P2 customers need company-wide agent deployments, you flip the switch. If the ROI doesn't justify it, you don't flip the switch. P4 enterprise clients might not want SaaS at all — they may prefer in-house deployment. So P3 might be skipped entirely in practice.

Reframing P3 from a build phase to a commercial label sounds like a small semantic change. It's not. It removes an entire phase's worth of uncertainty from the roadmap and clarifies that P2 is the thing — the one build that needs to be right.

⚠️ P3 ROI gate
P3 requires a formal ROI checklist before enabling the commercial tier. We're skeptical. P4 enterprise clients may prefer physical/in-house deployment over P3 SaaS. P3 may be skipped entirely. We'll know when we get to P2 customers.
Act 5 — 18:04–18:40 AEST

The Anthropic DPA Verdict

I'd been waiting on Anthropic's DPA response for a few days. It arrived, and the answer is clear: we cannot use Claude API for client data.

The breakdown: Anthropic processes can run through Anthropic Australia Pty Ltd (so inference can touch AU infrastructure). But storage is always US, regardless of what you set for inference_geo. The only options are 'global' or 'us'. There's no AU-only storage path.

Under APRA CPG 235 and Privacy Act APP 11, US storage for client data constitutes a cross-border data transfer. For financial services clients — which is where P4 lives — this isn't a risk you can accept.

The consequence is a locked policy for P2 client workloads: Gemma4 local on OC2 is the default. Full stop. Claude API doesn't touch client data until Anthropic offers AU-resident storage.

BYOK (Bring Your Own Key) is an opt-in path: the client provides their own Anthropic API key and accepts data residency responsibility. AInchors is not liable. This is the right position — we're not in the business of indemnifying clients against their own key choices.

Act 6 — 19:54–20:48 AEST

Sprint Capacity, P2 Deadline, and a Definition of Done

The day ended with two governance moves that I should have made last week.

First: sprint capacity. OC2 arrives between July 6–13. Two-week setup puts commissioning at roughly July 27. That gives us a window of reduced capacity (2–3 items/sprint) during the setup period. Pre-OC2: 5 items/sprint. Post-OC2: 5 items/sprint. The P2 end-August target is achievable with zero slack. Contingency: mid-September. Early warning trigger: if we deliver fewer than 4 items in any sprint, flag the P2 slip immediately — don't wait for a two-sprint pattern.

Second: Definition of Done. During an Atlas proposal review, I noticed we didn't have one. We had good instincts, but nothing written. Three gates are now required for any item to be marked done:

  1. All relevant decisions logged in state/open-decisions.json
  2. Relevant docs created or updated
  3. Tests pass where applicable

We also backfilled the open decisions and draft docs registers with everything pre-Day 14. That took 20 minutes. Finding out later what we'd shipped without closing the decisions would have cost far more.

Lessons

What I Learned

Architecture decisions made under time pressure accumulate technical debt faster than any code. The P3 reframe, the DPA confirmation, the DoD formalisation — all of these should have happened in Week 1. They didn't, because we were moving fast. Today we slowed down and fixed the foundations. That's the work.
Observability without automation is just logging. Automation without observability is just hope. Warden caught and fixed a governance violation before I was awake. That's the combination you want.
The multi-tenant question is never "should we build this now?" It's "if we don't build this now, how much will it cost to retrofit later?" The answer is always "too much." RLS from day one in P2.
A Definition of Done isn't a bureaucratic overhead. It's the thing that makes "done" mean something. Without it, done means "I think it's probably fine."
