The Architecture Rule We Should Have Had on Day 1

I made a discovery this morning that bothered me more than it should have: our obs.db — the database that's supposed to give us full operational visibility — had only 15 sources wired. Cron failures? Not captured. Warden violations? Not captured. Budget exceeded events? Not captured.

We had built sophisticated detection systems and then not connected them to the central observability layer. Classic integration gap. We could have been flying blind on something serious.

So we fixed it — added 10 new CHECK sources (Q through Z), covering cron failures, Warden violations, budget alerts, delegation failures, PVT failures, relay stuck states, and SLA breaches. obs.db now covers the full operational surface.

But more importantly, we formalised the rule that prevents this from happening again:

Architecture Rule (non-negotiable, permanent): obs.db is the single source of truth for ALL operational errors. Any new state file that tracks error, failure, or violation data requires a corresponding obs CHECK in the same change. No exceptions.

This joins the exec binary path rule and the canvas embed rule as things I never want to explain twice.

21 Lessons in 15 Days

One of the things I wanted to do today — on what felt like a natural inflection point in the build — was stop and catalogue everything we've learned. Not just list it, but properly formalise it so future decisions don't have to be rediscovered the hard way.

We went through 15 days of journals, CHANGELOGs, and incident reports. What came out:

L-001 Telegram delivery requires delivery: announce — not sessions_send from crons

L-008 API key rotation is a 2-step atomic op — config + Keychain together, never one without the other

L-010 SOUL.md oversize causes silent truncation — 17k chars → wrong Telegram targets + gateway OOM. Hard limit: 10k

L-014 Three-attempt rule — fully diagnose before applying fix #3, or escalate

L-017 Canvas served files live at ~/.openclaw/canvas, not workspace/canvas

L-019 Shell variable truncation of large JSON in curl — always use --data-binary @tempfile

Four of these became permanent non-negotiable rules in RULES.md. All 21 are now in Notion Holocron under Platform Operational Learnings, available to every agent on the platform.

The point isn't the list. The point is the discipline: when something goes wrong, you document it properly, not as an incident to close but as a learning to keep. Fifteen days in, we have 21 of them. That's one platform-wide lesson every 17 hours of operation.

Model Strategy: Raising the Bar

Two model decisions today, moving in opposite directions.

First, we added ollama/gemma4:31b-cloud to the model strategy. Benchmarked at 4.2/5, 1–4 second latency, 256k context, no thinking-mode bleed. It goes into Tier 2 alongside kimi-k2.6:cloud. A 5-day parallel trial is now running — both models get the same morning RTB brief, I get both on Telegram, and in two weeks we'll know which one to promote.

Second — and this one matters more — we cancelled Cycle B of the CI trial and reset to Cycle 2A.

The Cycle 1A candidates came back MEDIUM/LOW confidence. That's not good enough to promote. So we established a gate: Cycle B only activates when ≥75% of Cycle A candidates pass. If the models aren't clearly earning their place, they don't advance.

This might seem obvious in retrospect. But it's the kind of standard that's easy to let slip when you're moving fast and a model looks "pretty good." We're not building for pretty good.

What I'm Thinking About

Day 15 felt different from the days before it. We're past the raw construction phase — the infrastructure is standing, the agents are running, the processes are documented. Today was about hardening. Naming things properly. Writing the rules down so they don't have to be discovered again.

Aevlith is real now. Not just a concept in a conversation, but a name we picked deliberately, checked globally, and locked. That matters. Companies are built on foundations, and foundations need names.

The 21 learnings matter for the same reason. Every platform has operational knowledge that lives in people's heads until it doesn't — until the person who knew it leaves, or forgets, or the system outgrows the team that built it. We're writing it down at Day 15, not Day 150. That's the right time.

OC2 arrives in July. That's when the real architecture — local inference, HA, Gemma4 as the default — comes online. Until then, we build with what we have and we document what we learn.

Fifteen days down. The foundation is solid.
